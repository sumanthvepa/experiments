# Chapter 1: The Nature of Probability Theory

## Background
There are three distict aspects of any theory, including Probability, the subject of this book, that one must understand:

1. The formal logic of the theory
2. The intutiion behind the theory, and
3. The applications of the theory.

All three are important to fully understand a theory.

### Formal Logic
Like any mathematical theory, probability deals with the relations between concepts that are themselves undefined (i.e. they are axioms of the the theory.)

This is similar to geometry, for example where the notion of a point is not defined.  It is taken as an axiom of the theory. What is rigorously defined are the relationships between axioms. 

> **Axioms of Probability**: The axioms of modern probability are the Kolmogorov axioms. These are introduced later in the book (I think.) These are described on the Wikipedia page on [Probability Axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_axioms).

### Intuition
While in games like chess or geometry, the formal rules leave the axiom undefined, in practice one develops a strong intution of what is meant. For example, everyone has a notioon of what a point is even though it is left undefined in geometry. One also develops intutition for derived concepts such as a line. One needs to develop a similar intuition for concepts in probability. This comes with experience of the field.

### Applications
The application of a theory should be made in a way that fits the problem being addressed. First, start with a problem, chosse a model from based on the theory that you think best fits the problem, then judge the model by its usefulness not its ultimate truth. The example given here is from Physics. A model which treats an object as a rigid body cannot technically be true, since truly rigid bodies do not exist in nature. Nevertheless, a model based on rigid bodies is very useful in many situations in mechanics and it gives good answers. Simiarly, when applying probability theory to applications, its more important to judge whether a model derived from theory, is useful in the context of the problem than to determine its emperical truth.

> Take a coin toss. We assume that there are only two possible outcomes {H,T}. In reality, the coin may, on very rare occassions, land on its edge. We also assume that each toss is independent of the other. Which in real life is not literally true. It is entirely possible that if you fully understood how the coin was picked up and then tossed again and you had the ability to model every detail of biomechanics, and the fluid mechanics of air, you could exactly predict how the coind would land based on the previous outcome (and a bunch of other inputs.) But a model that disregards these details is still an extremely useful one in predicting the behaviour of coin tosses in the real world.
